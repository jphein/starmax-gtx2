Starmax GTX2 · custom-firmware track

Teardown — guided walkthrough

A step-by-step guide to safely opening a spare GTX2 and capturing the photos we need to find a firmware-recovery path. Tick each shot as you take it — your progress is saved on this device.

✅
Verdict: conditional GO — on the spare only.

A sacrificial unit means the worst case is a dead spare, never your daily watch — and it lets us prove recovery, which is the gate that was missing before. Keep your daily driver on stock.

⛔
Hard rule: no custom flash until backup + restore is proven.

We read the stock flash to a golden.bin and prove we can write it back first. The flash is an external SPI-NOR chip, so a programmer can always re-flash it — that's the safety net.

00 Before you start

  • Use a SPARE. Never the daily driver.
  • Power the watch off before opening.
  • Opening voids water resistance (gasket/adhesive won't reseal well).
  • Go slow near the LiPo battery and the ribbon/FPC cables — don't puncture the cell or tear a flex.
  • Have a macro-capable camera and good, raking light (side-lit shows silkscreen text + pads best).
  • Tools: plastic spudger/pry, fine tweezers, small Phillips if screwed. (Later, for recovery: an SWD probe — ST-Link/J-Link/CMSIS-DAP — or a SPI-NOR programmer + a clip for the flash chip.)

01 Open the case — INSPECT FIRST, don't pry yet

Direction is not yet settled — decide it from the closed unit, not by guessing. The FCC photos show the watch fully disassembled (back puck off in Fig 1, front module off in Fig 2), so they reveal what's on which face but not the intended service seam. Evidence leans FRONT (the component side with the SoC/flash/pads carries the two display/touch FPC connectors — Figs 12/13 — so it sits under the display), but that's an inference, not proof. So: look before you open.

⛔
These hold either direction: no heat gun; don't force a bonded AMOLED; mind the LiPo + FPCs.

Heat = heat pad / hair-dryer ≤70 °C only (a gun cooks the LiPo / delaminates the panel). Never pry against the AMOLED glass or the LiPo. Plastic tools only.

THE DECIDER — check the closed spare + photograph before prying:

  • Back: is there a removable plate? Look for screws (under the HR-sensor ring, a label, or rubber plugs) or a pry seam / gasket line around the rim. Back screws or a clean back seam ⇒ back-service (remove back → battery → possibly flip the PCB to reach the component side).
  • Front: is the glass/bezel a bonded display with no seam? That ⇒ front-service (heat + pick the display assembly out; it's tethered by the 2 display/touch FPCs).
  • Photograph CLOSED first: back, front, and the edge/seam — shot #1. Then report: screws (how many/what head) or a glued seam? which face has the removable cover? That settles the direction; we finalize this step from your photos.

Once you're in (either way): the target is the component side (SoC/flash + 2 FPC connectors). Watch the 2 display/touch FPCs (flip the latch, don't pull the ribbon) and the LiPo. Then macro the pad cluster NW of the SoC (Fig-13 red arrow) — the SWD/test-point candidate.

03 The recovery gate — what we're hunting for

A wired path to read + rewrite the flash. Any one of these is enough:

  • SWD debug pads — a 2–6 pad cluster near the SoC: SWDIO, SWCLK, usually GND, sometimes nRESET / VCC / SWO.
  • UART — 2–3 pads; the TX line idles high and bursts at boot (~2 Mbaud). Useful for logs, maybe a bootloader.
  • The flash chip directly — XT25F128F is an external SPI-NOR. A clip or flying leads on its pins let a programmer read/write it regardless of any SoC lock. This is the fallback that makes a permanent brick unlikely.
  • Any isolated gold pad or via = a candidate test point — photograph it.

04 Recovery sequence — the safety gate

We do R1 → R3 before any custom flash. This is non-negotiable.

  • R1Establish wired accessSWD probe + OpenOCD "connect under reset", read the M33 IDCODE (proves SWD is alive, not fused). Dead SWD → SPI-NOR programmer on the flash.
  • R2Back up the stock flash → golden.binread the full chip twice, compare, verify the size, keep off-device copies. Do this FIRST.
  • R3Prove restorewrite golden.bin back and confirm the watch boots. This is the hinge: if a later flash bricks it, you re-flash golden.
  • R4Custom flash — only after R3 passesflash the modified image; on any failure, restore golden. Start app-only (keeps the stock recovery/bootloader untouched).

05 Hand off the photos

  • Save every shot into docs/teardown-photos/ in the project.
  • Tell your assistant they're in — it reads them, identifies the SoC / flash / debug pads, and routes to the firmware agent to turn the pad layout into the concrete R1 wiring.
  • Then we source the right probe/programmer and start at R1. Nothing gets flashed before the backup + restore is proven.

🧯
Why a brick is unlikely (with the right tool).

The flash is external SPI-NOR, so a programmer reads/writes it directly — bypassing any SWD lock or SoC read-protection. Secure-boot (if the eFuse is burned) only gates the recovery bootloader; an app-only mod leaves that untouched. The real risks left: the flash may be leadless (WSON — trickier to clip), and opening the watch ends its water resistance.
